Do Vision Models Fail Like Humans?

A systematic study of directional perceptual error alignment across classical illusions and modern vision architectures

Jules Herrscher · Matisse Van Schalkwijk · Lysandre Costes · Imane Oujja

EPFL, CS-503 Visual Intelligence: Machines and Minds

Code

Abstract

Visual illusions are stimuli on which the human visual system makes systematic, reproducible errors, and are arguably the cleanest probe we have into the inductive biases of human vision. Prior work has shown that some deep vision models also misperceive certain illusions, but most studies measure raw accuracy and confuse “the model is wrong” with “the model is wrong in the same direction as a human.”

We present a systematic study quantifying, across five families of classical illusions and four model families, the directional alignment between human and model perceptual errors. That is, not whether models fail, but whether they fail like us. Our key contribution is a unified Human Error Alignment Score (HEAS) that distinguishes human-like illusion susceptibility from generic non-robustness, and an analysis of which architectural and training choices push models toward human-like perception. On LLaVA-1.5 we also study preference alignment with DPO [16] and SymMPO (symmetric polarity optimization), motivated when DPO raised HEAS but showed semantic overfitting.

Key results: human-like illusion bias is category-specific, not a global property of “deep vision.” Supervised ViTs can reach high HEAS on geometric length (up to ≈ 0.92) while staying near the floor on color and angle (often ≈ 0.08–0.14); contrastive encoders remain largely anti-illusory on geometry (HEAS ≈ 0.12). Preference-tuned LLaVA can raise reported illusory rates: DPO reaches mean HEAS ≈ 0.91 on evaluated VLM cells, but saliency and control checks suggest this can reflect language shortcuts as much as visual grounding.

Introduction

Visual illusions are signatures of the inductive biases the human visual system uses to resolve ambiguous inputs. For visual intelligence, the central question is whether modern models exhibit these same systematic errors when trained on natural images.

Prior work has answered “sometimes yes” for individual illusions and individual models [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], and recent benchmarks [6], [7] report that vision–language models perform far below humans on illusion VQA. Two questions remain only partially answered:

  1. Direction, not just accuracy. Most evaluations measure whether the model gives the physically correct answer. This confuses two opposite findings: a model that says “the lines are equal” is less human-like than one that says “the top line is longer” (the human illusion). Being right for the wrong reason is not human-like, and neither is being wrong in an arbitrary direction.
  2. Cross-architecture systematicity. Existing studies typically test one illusion family on one model class. We lacked a per-illusion, per-architecture map of which biases transfer from human to artificial vision.

We pose three concrete research questions:

  • RQ1: On which illusion categories do contemporary vision models exhibit the same directional bias as humans?
  • RQ2: Does this human-likeness correlate with architecture or pretraining objective (supervised, contrastive, self-supervised, language-aligned)?
  • RQ3: For illusions where models do not match humans, what are they doing instead: failing randomly, using basic data patterns, or following a different bias?

Why this matters

Illusions are a way to reverse-engineer how vision works. If a model falls for the same illusions we do, it probably shares our visual biases; if it does not, its internal logic may be fundamentally different. Standard benchmarks such as ImageNet-C [19] or ObjectNet [20] test how models handle blurry or noisy photos; illusions test their core priors: a signal that many robustness studies ignore. This also matters for VLM hallucination, as recent results [7] show: we need to know whether a model fails because it has a human-like visual prior or because it repeats a memorized caption. A per-category alignment score gives a concrete way to compare which models behave more like human vision.

Our contributions consist of:

  • The Human Error Alignment Score (HEAS) for comparing model vs. human illusory-response rates, not accuracy alone.
  • A unified benchmark across five illusion categories, eight models, and parametric psychometric sweeps.
  • Evidence that supervised, contrastive, and language-aligned models diverge systematically in how they fail.
  • VLM alignment on base LLaVA with DPO [16] and SymMPO (symmetric polarity preference optimization).

Related Work

Illusions in vision models

Gomez-Villa et al. [1], [2] showed CNNs trained for low-level tasks reproduce certain brightness and color illusions; Ward [4] reported similar effects for shape illusions; Sun and Dekel [3] found illusion-like responses to the scintillating grid in ImageNet networks; Benjamin et al. [11] measured shared illusions at small scale. These studies are typically per-illusion, per-model, and report accuracy rather than directional alignment.

Illusions in VLMs

Zhang et al. [5] first probed VLMs on illusions through language. IllusionVQA [6] and HallusionBench [7] are large benchmarks reporting that VLMs underperform humans, but they treat illusions as a failure mode (model vs. ground truth) rather than as an alignment probe (model vs. human). Our framing inverts this.

Human–machine alignment

Geirhos et al. [12], [13] compared human and model error patterns under naturalistic distortions and showed models are texture-biased while humans are shape-biased. We extend this from naturalistic distortions to designed perceptual stimuli where the human “error pattern” is itself the object of study. Our model panel includes supervised CNNs and ViTs [8], contrastive encoders (CLIP [10], DINOv2 [9]), and LLaVA-1.5 [17] as a vision–language model.

What we add:

  1. A directional alignment score, not raw accuracy.
  2. Coverage of five illusion categories on the same model panel.
  3. Architecture-vs.-training disentanglement.
  4. A reusable stimulus generator and open materials to reproduce the full benchmark.

Method

We treat illusion evaluation as a directional alignment problem: each model answers a three-way probe (physically correct, human-illusory, other) on matched illusion/control pairs. Below we justify stimulus design, architecture-specific probing, the HEAS metric, and VLM alignment with DPO and SymMPO (from base LLaVA), evaluated in Experiments.

Stimulus design

We evaluate whether vision models exhibit the same directional errors as humans on classical illusions. Each trial presents a three-way choice: physically correct, human-illusory, or other (any response that is neither the physical truth nor the human-illusory direction, e.g. refusals, off-topic answers, or probe failures). Programmatic generators yield about 4,400 synthetic illusion–control pairs across nine illusion types with parametric sweeps; scene-level impossible objects come from IllusionVQA [6] and HallusionBench [7] (~435 items); motion-from-static illusions follow Sun and Dekel [3]. For impossible scenes we evaluate only VLMs and contrastive models: these items are language-heavy VQA without matched visual controls, so CNN/ViT linear-probe protocols do not apply.

Matched controls and sanity checks

Each programmatic trial is an illusion / control pair: the control removes the biasing cue while keeping layout and luminance. We only score an illusion image if the model answers correct on its paired control (except impossible-scene items, which often lack true controls). CNN/ViT probes must reach ≥80% accuracy on controls before illusion scoring; CLIP and LLaVA use the same text prompt on both images of a pair.

Probing protocols and bias controls

  • CNN / ViT (supervised): Frozen ImageNet backbone + 3-way linear probe trained on synthetic non-illusion images only (no illusion labels in training). Vision is tested via frozen features; this avoids label leakage but may miss illusion-specific fine-tuning effects.
  • CLIP (contrastive): Zero-shot image–text matching with short descriptions tailored to each illusion (e.g. “looks longer”, “looks darker”) rather than naming the effect. The same three phrases score both illusion and control images.
  • DINOv2 (self-supervised): Image–image similarity to reference patches per answer class (vision-only; no text tower).
  • LLaVA-1.5 [17] (language-aligned): Multiple-choice VQA. Following HallusionBench’s diagnosis of position bias [7], each trial randomizes the A/B/C option order and draws one of two framings: neutral (“answer about the image”) vs. name-blind (explicitly avoids illusion names). Bias is mitigated across the dataset by varying order/framing per stimulus; the model is queried once per image per pass (same prompt on the paired control). IllusionVQA items use dataset-provided questions when available.

HEAS metric and validity

HEAS(m, c) = 1 − | pmodelillusory(m, c) − phumanillusory(c) |

pmodelillusory is the fraction of illusion trials (after control filtering) where the model chooses the human-illusory answer. phumanillusory(c) is one reference rate per illusion category used in the score. HEAS = 1 means the model’s illusory-response rate matches that reference; HEAS ≈ 0 means opposite or absent illusion bias. Category aggregation is a deliberate simplification (see Limitations).

Design choices and alternatives

We chose synthetic illusion images for controlled sweeps and reproducibility; alternatives such as photo-realistic renders or in-the-wild images would improve ecological validity but complicate parametric psychometrics. A binary correct/incorrect probe would be simpler but cannot separate human-like from anti-illusory failures,hence the three-way design. For VLMs we use multiple-choice text rather than free-form answers to reduce parsing ambiguity; for CNNs/ViTs we use linear probes on frozen features rather than end-to-end fine-tuning on illusions, which would conflate alignment with illusion-specific overfitting.

Preference alignment overview

Base LLaVA-1.5 [17] often under-reports illusory answers relative to our reference rates. We first applied direct preference optimization (DPO) [16]: roughly 1,000 preference pairs where human-illusory multiple-choice responses are preferred over physically correct ones on illusion images (reversed on controls), with a LoRA [18] adapter on the language backbone (rank 16 on the legacy training run). The released checkpoint is Jukess/llava_illusions_dpo on Hugging Face. DPO raised category-level HEAS, but qualitative analysis suggested semantic overfitting. The frozen vision encoder cannot change what the model sees, so the language head may learn to output “illusory” labels from prompt wording rather than from visual evidence.

We therefore developed SymMPO (Symmetric Multimodal Preference Optimization), also trained from base LLaVA-1.5. Public weights: Matisse6410/LlaVa-1.5-SDPO on Hugging Face. SymMPO keeps the same illusory-alignment goal but adds polarity-inverted prompts on the same image, margin consistency between those prompts, anchored preference regularisation, factual control VQA to limit catastrophic forgetting, and a trainable multimodal projector so visual features can be remapped into text space. Full loss and data details follow; HEAS re-evaluation is reported in the SymMPO benchmark results.

SymMPO dataset (polarity-paired preferences)

Training pairs are drawn from programmatic geometric, color, angle, and motion illusions (no impossible-scene items). For each illusion image we include two opposing text prompts on the same static image:

  • Original polarity: e.g. “Which line appears longer?” with chosen = human-illusory answer and rejected = physically correct / equal.
  • Inverted polarity: e.g. “Which line appears shorter?” with chosen and rejected flipped so the human-illusory interpretation stays consistent with the image.

Approximately 20% of samples are non-illusion control VQA: factual questions (“How many lines are shown?”) with the correct answer preferred, to limit catastrophic forgetting of basic VQA.

SymMPO training setup

Training starts from base LLaVA-1.5 [17]:

  • Frozen CLIP vision tower: preserves pretrained visual features.
  • Trainable multimodal projector: the MLP that maps vision tokens into the language model space.
  • LoRA on the language backbone [18]: rank 64 on attention and MLP layers (vision layers excluded).
  • Reference model: log-probabilities for the DPO ratio are computed from the same weights with adapters disabled, avoiding a second full model in memory.

The collator applies symbol demonstration (option letters A/B/C mapped to ♣/♠/♦) and dynamic option shuffling, analogous in spirit to the evaluation-time MCQ debiasing inspired by HallusionBench [7].

SymMPO loss

Instead of a single DPO term, SymMPO minimises a four-part objective. Because the image I is held fixed within each term, standard DPO partition functions cancel in the multimodal setting:

L(θ) = LDPO_m(θ) + γ LSymmetric(θ) + λ LMargin(θ) + η LAncPO(θ)
  • LDPO_m: standard multimodal DPO log-sigmoid loss on the original prompt (β = 0.1).
  • LSymmetric: identical DPO loss on the polarity-inverted prompt (γ = 1.0).
  • LMargin: mean squared error between the original and inverted preference margins, encouraging symmetric relative preference strength (λ = 0.5).
  • LAncPO: negative log-likelihood of chosen completions on both polarities, stabilising absolute log-probabilities during training (η = 0.1).

Experiments

Experimental setup

We evaluate eight publicly available vision models spanning four training paradigms. These models serve as baselines representative of how the field typically obtains visual representations, not because any one is a perfect stand-in for human vision, but because together they let us ask whether human-like illusion susceptibility tracks architecture scale, supervised ImageNet training, contrastive language alignment, or VLM instruction tuning.

Category-dependent alignment

Human-like directional bias is not universal. It is reliable only for geometric length in supervised CNNs/ViTs, largely absent for color and angle in those families, and selectively restored by language-aligned models (especially LLaVA + DPO) on color, motion, and geometry.

Geometric alignment does not transfer: the same ResNet that reaches HEAS 0.87 on length scores 0.08 on color, while humans still report strong simultaneous-contrast illusions. Thus “fails like humans” must be stated per category, not per model globally.

HEAS clusters by training interface more than by depth. Supervised models share high geometric / low color-angle profiles. Contrastive models are anti-illusory on geometry (HEAS 0.12 for CLIP and DINOv2). VLMs with DPO achieve the highest mean alignment (~0.91 mean HEAS over evaluated cells).

Saliency and interpretability

Grad-CAM [14], ViT attention rollout [21], and LLaVA token-level maps offer hypotheses for why models misalign, not causal proof. DPO increases saliency on fins (Müller-Lyer) and contrast patches (simultaneous contrast), consistent with higher illusory rates but not demonstrating human-identical processing.

Ablation: VLM alignment (DPO and SymMPO)

Base LLaVA-1.5 starts with patchy illusion alignment: strong on some scene and angle items, weak on color and motion (see overview tables above). We asked whether preference training could raise illusory-response rates without retraining the vision backbone. We first applied DPO [16], which moves HEAS sharply upward; qualitative follow-up then motivated SymMPO when those gains looked better on paper than in the image.

DPO: what we tried and what we found

We fine-tuned LLaVA-1.5 with DPO on roughly 1,000 illusion/control preference pairs (illusory answers preferred on illusion images, reversed on controls; LoRA rank 16 on the language model; frozen CLIP encoder). Weights are public as Jukess/llava_illusions_dpo (llava_1.5_dpo in our benchmark).

What improved. DPO was our first evidence that a VLM can be steered toward human-like illusory rates. Mean HEAS over evaluated VLM cells rose from 0.62 (base) to 0.91 (DPO). The largest jumps were where base LLaVA under-reported illusion most: color +0.52, motion +0.61, and geometric +0.29. Psychometric sweeps show the effect clearly on Müller-Lyer and simultaneous contrast: the model’s illusory-response curve moves upward when illusion strength increases, not only on a single prompt template.

What worried us. High HEAS alone is not enough if the model is not using the image. Follow-up analysis and saliency checks (supplementary figures below) pointed to three issues that motivate SymMPO:

  1. Perception gap. With the vision tower frozen, alignment can only change how features are described, not what is perceived.
  2. Semantic overfitting. The model often answers “illusory” in a systematic way, as if it learned to guess from prompt wording rather than to see the illusion.
  3. Blind high HEAS. Green cells on the heatmap can mask a “hallucination of alignment”: satisfying the preference loss without reliable grounding in visual evidence.

Conclusion and Limitations

Comparing directional illusory-response rates with HEAS, not raw accuracy, shows that modern vision models do not “fail like humans” in one global sense. Alignment appears only in specific category×model cells; nowhere does a single architecture track human bias across all five illusion families.

The clearest pattern is category specificity. Supervised CNNs and ViTs can reach high HEAS on geometric length (best cells ≈ 0.92) yet often sit near the floor on color and angle (≈ 0.08–0.14), where humans remain strongly illusory. Vision–language models are uneven out of the box; DPO [16] pushes mean HEAS on evaluated VLM cells to ≈ 0.91, especially on color and motion, but control checks and saliency suggest part of that lift may reflect language shortcuts as much as visual grounding. SymMPO has a low HEAS when controlling for passed answers.

These differences line up less with parameter count than with how representations were learned. Supervised models share a high-geometric / low-color fingerprint; contrastive encoders (CLIP, DINOv2) can pass controls yet stay anti-illusory on geometry (~0.12). Preference tuning on LLaVA moves reported illusion rates more than swapping among similar-scale supervised backbones, a reminder that the training interface matters as much as the backbone class.

Where models miss humans, the errors are structured, not random: correct-avoidant color responding, anti-illusory geometry, other-collapse on difficult angle trials, and, before control filtering, illusory saturation on some VLM items without reliable control competence. Psychometric curves and saliency maps help read these modes qualitatively; they are not evidence of human-identical internal processing.

Several limits bound how far to generalize. HEAS uses one reference illusory rate per category, so a model can match the score while diverging on curve shape or individual illusions; we judge psychometric shape by eye, not rank correlation. The evaluation grid is sparse in places (e.g. no ResNet on motion; no CNN/ViT on impossible scenes), and empty heatmap cells mean unevaluated, not zero alignment. Each model family is probed differently (linear probes, CLIP zero-shot, DINOv2 matching, debiased LLaVA MCQ per HallusionBench [7]), so low HEAS may partly reflect protocol mismatch. Impossible-scene benchmarks [6], [7] lack the tight illusion–control pairing of our programmatic stimuli; alignment on LLaVA with DPO and SymMPO is a case study on one VLM [17]. Grad-CAM [14] and occlusion maps [15] show where models look, not why they answer as they do.

The fair claim is cautious: under explicit directional metrics, some models align with human error patterns on some illusion types, and many do not. Filling the sparse grid, and adding rank-based psychometric statistics would sharpen that picture further.
